Introduction


The co-culture aspect makes this project unique and allows us to bring the properties of cyanobacteria and E. coli together to form the biomanufacturing unit. Although the organisms have been modelled individually, the co-culture brings forth new properties and interactions that may offer additional insights and alternative techniques to optimize the production process.


Two techniques have been used to model the co-culture - SteadyCom and Dynamic Modelling. SteadyCom assumes a community steady state that allows us to predict the stability of the coo-culture and long-term interactions between the organisms. The dynamic modelling algorithm, developed by the INSA-UPS Toulouse iGEM Team, allows us to track the development of the co-culture over time. Together, these two models built on slightly different assumptions can offer a wide range of insights.

SteadyCom


Building the Community Model of S. elongatus 2973 and E coli


SteadyCom uses a joint community model which is made by putting together the GSMâ€™s of the individual species in the community. The individual models used to build the community model of our co culture were the modified versions of iML1515 and the TUEX 2973 model that were built to match KJK01 and the sucrose producing cyanobacteria. All the steadycom simulations including the creation of the joint model was done using the COBRA toolbox on MATLAB. During the process of creating the joint model however, we found that due to lack of documentation on SteadyCom and standardisation of GSM models, we faced many issues. We eventually troubleshot our issues after experimenting with joint models of just E coli and cyanobacteria and figuring out the intricacies of the joint model. This led to us deciding to create a checklist that we believe would greatly help future users of SteadyCom on COBRA navigate their way through creating joint community models and troubleshooting. You can read more about that on our contributions page here: Contributions.


Butanol is a secondary metabolite, so when just growth rate is optimized for in SteadyCom, butanol would not be produced as it would be taking away carbon flux from the growth pathways. In the actual co-culture, the induction by IPTG would force carbon flux through the butanol production pathway to produce butanol. To simulate this we set a lower bound for the butanol exchange reaction which would constrain the model to have flux through the butanol production pathway in E coli. The particular value for the lower bound was obtained through FBA at different percentages of optimal growth rate. You can read about the exact method we used to set the lower bound here: Appendix. The higher the percentage of optimal growth, the lower the butanol productivity.


The carbon dioxide uptake rate of the cyanobacteria was set at -12.2mmol/gDW/hour. Salt stress was simulated by setting the sucrose produced by cyanobacteria as per the results of FBA at 90% cscB efficiency. The butanol production for E coli was set at 60% of optimal growth. Oxygen exchange between the two species was allowed but excess oxygen was not provided to the culture.

Identifying Key metabolites controlling interactions in the Co-culture


We knew that sucrose secreted by the cyanobacteria would play an important role in the interaction between the two species of the co culture as there is no other carbon source available for the E coli. Apart from sucrose, we ran SteadyCom FVA to identify other metabolites that could play crucial roles in the interactions of the consortium. SteadyCom FVA (Flux Variability Analysis) calculates the maximum and minimum values of flux through a set of reactions while upholding the optimal growth rate at steady state. By running FVA on the set of shuttle reactions of metabolites between the individual species and the community space, we identified those metabolites that are required to be taken up or given out by each species to maintain optimal community growth rate.

Results: Apart from sucrose, two other metabolites that were required to be given out by one organism and taken in by the other to grow optimally were carbon dioxide and oxygen. Carbon dioxide is given out by E coli and taken in by the cyanobacteria and is already an integral patt of the production process acting as the main carbon source for the entire process. Oxygen given out by the cyanobacteria could act as an important source for e coli, who prefer aerobic conditions for growth.

Apart from these, there were other metabolites such as sulfate, magnesium, phosphate, etc that both bacteria require to be supplied in the medium. This would imply that a shortage of these nutrients could lead to competition between the two species and de-stability of the co culture.


Varying sucrose productivity of the cyanobacteria


Hypothesis: Increasing sucrose productivity of cyanobacteria will result in the increase of butanol yield from the co culture.


Currently according to the salt stress model, cyanobacteria is producing about 0.3 mmol of sucrose per hour per gDW. But we decided to vary the sucrose productivity of cyanobacteria and model its effects on the butanol yield of the co culture. This was done by changing the flux of sucrose given out by cyanobacteria per gDW.

As the sucrose productivity of cyanobacteria increases:

  3. The butanol produced per gDW of the co culture initially increases but after an optimal value of sucrose, it comes down

So for very high values of sucrose productivity (which is per gDW of the cyanobacteria), there is very little cyanobacteria in the culture and hence not a lot of sucrose for the E. coli to take up and produce butanol. A more detailed explanation of this result is given in the appendix.


Varying butanol productivity/ % of optimal growth rate for E coli

Hypothesis: Increase in the butanol productivity of E coli will result in an increase of the butanol yield of the co culture.

Apart from the sucrose productivity of Cyanobacteria, the butanol productivity of E coli is another important factor that will determine the amount of butanol produced by the co culture. As per how induction by IPTG is simulated in the model, a higher butanol productivity is correlated with the e coli growing at a lower percentage of its optimal/maximum growth rate at the given conditions. In reality, increasing butanol productivity could be achieved by a higher IPTG concentration, or by increasing the efficiency of the butanol pathway. This would redirect carbon flux away from growth and towards producing butanol.


Insert Graphs 7,8,9

Observations:
As butanol productivity of E coli increases:

  1. The growth rate of the co-culture remains the same
  2. The butanol produced per gDW of the co culture increases
  3. The value of optimum sucrose productivity did not vary significantly at different levels of butanol productivity

Conclusions:
The maximum specific growth rate of the co culture is primarily controlled by the cyanobacteria hence will majorly only depend on the Co2 uptake rate and sucrose productivity. When butanol productivity of the E coli was increased, its relative abundance in the co culture decreased. This means that since there was less E coli in the co culture, it could take up more sucrose per gDW such that it produced more butanol while still growing at the growth rate of the cyanobacteria.

Increasing the butanol productivity of e coli does increase the butanol productivity of the co culture. This is as each the E coli are now diverting more flux into the butanol producing pathway and hence are converting a higher fraction of the sucrose given out by the cyanobacteria into butanol. However, this comes at the cost of decreasing relative abundance of E coli in the co culture. While running steadycom, it was not possible for the E coli to grow below around 50% of its optimal growth rate. From which we can infer that the butanol productivity cannot be arbitrarily increased as after a point the co culture would not be able to maintain a steady state.


Varying Oxygen levels to test aerobic vs micro aerobic vs anaerobic


Hypothesis: Decreasing oxygen levels will result in an increase of the butanol yield of the co culture.

As seen in the FVA results above, oxygen was one of the important metabolites being exchanged by the cyanobacteria and E coli. It is to be noted that cyanobacteria are producing more oxygen than the E coli need and the co culture as a whole would have a positive production of oxygen (see appendix for more). This means that our simulations would be in an aerobic environment as far as the E coli are concerned. However, based on discussions with experts and FBA results for E coli, we realised that anaerobic or even microaerobic conditions might result in a higher butanol yield. Thus we decided to look at how lowering oxygen levels in the co culture would affect butanol yield. We did this by setting decreasing bounds to how much oxygen the E coli could take up per gDW.

Appendix


Simulating IPTG Induction for the production of Butanol


Similar to how it was done in FBA, IPTG was induced by setting a bound for E coli to grow at a certain percentage of its optimal growth rate and diverting flux through the butanol production pathway. However due to the nature of the SteadCom algorithm, this is not as simple to do as in FBA. One cannot optimize for butanol within steadycom as the objective function is set to be the community growth rate, and further the sucrose intake by E coli, on which the optimal growth rate depends, varies. The method we used to overcome the problem was the following:

  1. SteadyCom is run on the joint model while simulating salt stress but not IPTG. The butanol production in this case is usually 0 as growth is being optimized
  2. Sucrose intake by the E coli per gDW from the SteadyCom result is used as the lower bound for an FBA with the E coli model
  3. Growth rate is set to a percent of optimal at this level of sucrose intake and butanol is optimized for using FBA
  4. Butanol flux gotten from FBA in this manner is set as a lower bound in the joint model and SteadyCom is run once again
  5. This would lead to the production of butanol even though community growth rate is still being optimized for
  6. The true percent of optimal growth rate the E coli is growing at can be found by comparing the results from the final SteadyCom run to FBA
  7. This can be done at different percentages of optimal growth (relating inversely to butanol productivity) as well as different levels of sucrose productivity, oxygen, etc.

Detailed explanation for optimal sucrose productivity


For high values of sucrose productivity, the max specific growth rate values of the co culture are low. This is as the cyanobacteria cannot grow fast while producing high amounts of sucrose per gDW. In turn, due to the steady state conditions, the E coli is also constrained to grow at a low growth rate. (In reality, if the E coli do grow at a higher growth rate, they will quickly deplete the sucrose in the medium.) To maintain this condition, the relative abundance of the E coli in the co culture must be higher such that per gDW of E coli, there is not much sucrose available and the E coli is constrained to grow at a lower growth rate.
This makes sense biologically as well. Initially the E coli will grow fast and deplete the sucrose until it has to slow its growth rate down, but this results in it having a high abundance at steady state.

As sucrose productivity increases the actual butanol produced per gDW of E coli is decreasing, but its relative biomass in the co culture is increasing. These two competing effects lead to an optimum of sucrose productivity for the butanol productivity per gDW of the co culture.

Introduction

Mathematical modelling has become an integral part of almost all projects dealing with metabolic engineering. Information starting from the dynamics of our system, to what genetic modifications can further our project can be obtained from the right mathematical models. These models help as starting points for the wet-lab experiments as they give a rough idea of where to target your attention.

SynBactory being heavily dependent on organismâ€™s growths, behaviours and interactions, along with metabolic networks and its possible manipulations, is a project that can benefit a lot from mathematical models.

The main objectives were:
  1. Predict growth rates of our individual organisms in pure cultures
  2. Predict growth rates under different stresses
  3. Identify the important interactive metabolites in the co culture
  4. Identify targets to over express or knockout in each organism for improved yields of sucrose and butanol

The core technique of our modelling attempts is a constraint based modelling technique called Flux Balance Analysis, in short FBA.Â We have used the COBRA package in MATLAB1 for almost all our models.


Flux Balance Analysis

FBA is a steady state analysis algorithm that is widely used in metabolic predictions and optimisations. FBA assumes the organism to be in a metabolically steady state i.e., there is no accumulation of metabolites within the organism.2 To perform FBA we require a metabolic model of the organism. A metabolic model of an organism contains information on all (or most) reactions in an organism, the metabolites involved, the genes present in the organism, the Gene-Reaction-Relation, and a calculated biomass production reaction (growth rate reaction) that gives the growth rate when analysed.

The core elements of FBA are2:
  1. S Matrix or the stoichiometric matrix - This is a matrix of the order (number of metabolites) * (number of reactions)
  2. The objective function or c - This is a column vector that holds the information to attain the aim of the analysis, for example biomass optimisation
  3. Lower bounds and upper bounds - This is a set of values that help constrain the solution space
  4. Solution flux space - This is a set of fluxes that give the same objective function value

In an organism, it is normal to have the same metabolites participating in multiple reactions, and therefore it is safe to assume the number of reactions will be more than the number of metabolites. This can be compared to a system of linear equations with more equations than variables. Without constraints the solution space is infinite, so we introduce constraints in the form of bounds.


Equations of FBA and their solutions2 Constraints:
  • Sv = 0
  • Lower Bounds
  • Upper Bounds

Objective Function2:
  • C(transpose)v

Objective2:
  • Minimise or Maximise Objective function

Solution2:
  • Plausible flux distributions for the above mentioned objective function

If one has perturbed the metabolic network of the organism, then simple maximisation of growth rate does not give results that agree with experiments. MoMA is a widely used algorithm to identify solution sets that agree most with real life values.


Minimisation of Metabolic Adjustments

MoMA is an extension of Flux Balance Analysis that imposes more constraints to make the model as close to reality as possible, albeit it isnâ€™t the best at doing so. The logic of MoMA is very simple - minimise the euclidean distance (L2 norm) between the wild-type flux distribution and the flux distribution of the modified organism in the flux vector space3.


The algorithm3:

  1. Find the wild-type/base strain flux distribution using FBA for maximized growth rates
  2. Simulate the deletion, over-expression or any stress
  3. Impose an additional constraint that minimizes the L2 norm of the solution and the wild type flux; it would look something like this - ||v_wild - v_mod||^2 is minimised.
  4. Run FBA multiple times to find such a v_mod.

Fascinating thing is this algorithm quite literally translates into the name of the technique.

Here we know what the modifications are that we need to make in order to get the strain we need, but what if we need to find further modifications that would help increase the yield of the product, simple FBA or MoMA will not suffice for this purpose. This is where we start using techniques like FSEOF and OptKnock.


Flux Scanning based on Enforced Objective Flux (FSEOF)

This algorithm was developed to identify gene targets that can be amplified for increased production of selected metabolites or even increased growth rates. We have used this technique to identify the reactions and further genes that we can over-express to increase sucrose production in cyanobacteria and butanol production in E. coli.

To understand FSEOF imagine the whole organism as a big city, the reactions as roads, and the flux through the reactions as the traffic in each road. Now consider evacuating this city as our objective, then the roads which lead to the exits will start carrying more traffic. If we can widen these roads we can in theory get more people out. If evacuating this city was another way of saying - maximise the production of a particular metabolite, then widen the roads can be considered as amplifying the corresponding reactions.


In the form of an algorithm it would look something like this4:

  1. Find maximum growth under media conditions using FBA
  2. Find the maximum production of the metabolite using FBA
  3. Constraint the production of the metabolite to some fraction of the maximum metabolite production value
  4. Run FBA for maximum growth rate
  5. Calculate the product of the metabolite production value for a given constraint and the corresponding growth rate value. This would help rank the target later on
  6. Plot how the flux values of each reaction changes as we increase the fractional value of the constraint on the metabolite

Since FBA and all of its extensions work with reactions and not genes we will have to trace the reactions back to its controlling genes and see if those genes are responsible for any other process that could end up killing the organism or hindering its growth if amplified.


OptKnock

OptKnock is an algorithm that find and suggests which set of reactions to remove from a metabolic network to obtain a mutant that will produce a particular target of interest at a higher rate than the wild-type strain5. The logic is pretty simple -

  1. You find the set of reactions that you want to get knockouts from
  2. Change their bounds to 0 (this is how you simulate deletion in models) either one by one or in pairs or triplets
  3. Run FBA for maximised growth
  4. Once you have a set of reactions which when deleted increases the production of the target of interest you trace them back to their respective genes
  5. Once youâ€™ve identified the genes you eliminate all the synthetic lethals from the set. Synthetic lethals are essential genes that was required by the organism to live

Once youâ€™ve eliminated all the synthetic lethal you have a set of reactions and their corresponding genes which can be knocked out for enhanced production of the metabolite of interest.


SteadyCom

SteadyCom is an algorithm used to analyze the steady state behaviour of co-cultures, microbiomes and host-microbe interactions. Similar to FBA, SteadyCom assumes that there is no accumulation of metabolites and only looks at the fluxes of reactions. Apart from this, the crux of SteadyCom is the assumption that - at steady state (if a steady state exists) the growth rates of all the organisms is the same, so that one organism does not dominate and take over the entire population. Based on the constraints in the model, SteadyCom tries to maximize this community growth rate, and provides results including the biomass ratios of the organisms at steady state growth and fluxes of various reactions of the multi-species metabolic network.

The Logic:

  1. Create a joint model which has all the reactions of every organismâ€™s GSM and a separate community space to allow for the transfer of common extracellular metabolites between the separate extracellular compartments of the individual models
  2. The algorithm predicts steady state fluxes which maximizes the community growth rate, such that all organisms grow at this rate
  3. The fluxes of reactions out of individual organisms are multiplied by their biomass, so that abundances of organisms are taken into account in the interactions

Dynamic Modelling


Method

Dynamic modelling here involves using fluxes obtained from FBA to form and solve ordinary differential equations that predict the concentrations of metabolites and biomasses in the bioreactor over time. Essentially, these fluxes act as the first order time derivatives in the system of differential equations.

When we optimize a model using FBA, we get the fluxes of all the reactions that allow the model to optimize its objective function. So, given an initial concentration of a metabolite, the FBA flux gives the derivative of the concentration at that point. Using this derivative, we can calculate the concentration of the metabolite at the next time step, and use this as the new initial concentration. This process can be repeated iteratively within the required time frame.

The algorithm:

  1. Set the flux restrictions of the cyanobacteria uptake rates, of co2 and light, based on concentration of these present in the bioreactor available for uptake
  2. Optimize the cyanobacteria model with sucrose production as its objective using FBA. This will naturally make the biomass flux of the cyanobacteria set to its lower bound 0. Biomass flux set to zero, implies that the rate of growth of biomass is zero. Hence, the maximized sucrose flux obtained from this result is the theoretical maximum sucrose yield possible from the model within the given constraints
  3. It is known that reaching the theoretical maximum yields of sucrose production is not very biologically feasible. So it is assumed that the salt stress provided to force cyanobacteria to produce sucrose, allows it to export sucrose out at some fraction of the maximum sucrose yield calculated above. This is referred to as the percentage productivity of sucrose flux in the cyanobacteria
  4. This productivity rate, x% of max sucrose flux, is now set as the lower bound for the sucrose export reaction in the cyanobacteria model, and the objective function is changed to biomass flux. An FBA is performed to maximize the biomass flux given this new constraint of sucrose production
  5. The final results from this FBA are taken as the fluxes of the cyanobacteria model
  6. The same process is repeated for the E. coli model, where the available sucrose in the bioreactor comes from the cyanobacteria flux calculated above. Butanol production is first optimized to find the maximum flux possible, then as above, some productivity for this reaction is set and biomass is optimized
  7. All the fluxes from both the models provide the time derivatives of the corresponding metabolites, which are integrated and plotted
