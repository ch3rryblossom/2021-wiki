E Coli



Bidirectional Promoter Characterization


We plan to characterize the native bidirectional promoter of the csc operon which we submitted as the part BBa_K3971006. Since the cscK and cscB genes are transcribed from the opposite strand to cscA, a bidirectional promoter jointly regulates both sets of genes in the operon. We will characterize the activity of the promoter using fluorescent reporters (a yellow and blue fluorescent protein) which flank the promoter and their relative fluorescence will inform us about the strength of transcription in both directions. The characterization cassette for the promoter has been submitted as the composite part _.
Characterization of this promoter will allow us to experiment with other promoters based on the results and potentially increase sucrose consumption and consequently increase growth rate and butanol yields.


Genetic engineering based on modeling results


Our dry lab team used OptKnock and flux scanning based on enforced objective flux (FSEOF) in order to identify potential candidate genes and reactions to target in order to increase sucrose and butanol yields. In the future, we aim to perform these modifications in the lab and hope to optimize the co-culture and final yields to make the process industrially viable.

S. elongatus


Characterising native promoters

I) Construct assembly


We have shortlisted the native promoters - PcpcB-m6, PpsbA2 and PpsbA3 along with the well-characterised J23119 promoter (from the iGEM kit) and the lacUV5 promoter used by Dr Pakrasi’s lab as standard reference promoters.

We intend to incorporate a promoter into a cassette upstream of the fluorescent reporter sYFP2 and a terminator. This cassette is itself flanked by two sequences NS1A and NS1B (reference construct __) which are homologous to the first genomic neutral site of Synechococcus elongatus UTEX 2973, intended for homology-directed repair. We will prepare this composite part via extension and overlap PCR using primers we have designed. This part will be integrated into the pSL2680 plasmid. Note that the promoter will be flanked by restriction sites, allowing us to excise and replace it with other promoters without repeating the complicated assembly step.

On the other side of the plasmid is the gene Cpf1 coding for the Cas12a enzyme. Downstream on the gene are various spacers, and a lacZ cassette, flanked by Aar1 restriction sites. We will synthesise a short stretch of DNA (via overlap PCR of two oligos) to bind to a protospacer in NS1 of the genome. This sequence will be introduced into the plasmid in place of the lacZ cassette, and will allow for a CRISPR-Cas12a-mediated targeted break of the genomic DNA. The homology arms NS1A and B will then allow for HDR of the genome to include our composite part.

A very similar principle applies to the overexpression of native sps and spp genes (previous work has expressed sps and spp genes from Synechocystis PCC 6803 in Synechococcus elongatus UTEX 2973). These genes will be incorporated into the ____ plasmid, which already contains homology arms for neutral site 3 of the S. elongatus genome.

II) Triparental Mating


The UTEX 2973 strain is unfortunately not naturally transformable, posing a challenge to genetic engineering work. To characterise the promoters and sps and spp, we will need to introduce the plasmids via conjugation from E. coli DH5α. The plasmids of interest - pSL2680 and _____ - will be transformed into this strain, which will then undergo Kanamycin screening and blue-white screening to select for transformants with lacZ replaced.

For biosecurity reasons, DH5α is typically not capable of forming a conjugation pilus. It thus needs to acquire an F-plasmid from a helper strain (for this purpose, we will use E. coli HB101). This will carry not only the F-plasmid, but also other helper and mobiliser plasmids (pRL443 and pRL623) needed for safe conjugative transfer of pSL2680 and ___ (our plasmids of interest) into UTEX 2973.


III) Fluorescence-based characterisation


We will grow the cyanobacteria at different light intensities (100-1500 microeinsteins) and different carbon dioxide concentrations (0.04%-5%). Under each such pair of conditions, we will characterise the promoter activity by measuring the fluorescence of sYFP. We will also simulate a day-night cycle, in accordance with the cyanobacteria’s natural circadian rhythm, to see if it impacts the sustainability and stability of (i) the axenic culture and its sucrose production and (ii) the co-culture and its butanol production. This is of particular interest in the case of PpsbAII and PpsbAIII, which are promoters associated with the circadian rhythm. We will select a promoter yielding optimum performance and use it to express cscB in UTEX 2973.


Sucrose Assay


We will use Megazyme’s sucrose assay kit to quantify the concentration of sucrose in supernatant samples of our axenic cultures and our co-culture. By taking serial measurements using this colorimeteric assay, we can estimate the increase in sucrose production with time in a cyanobacterial culture (UTEX 2793-cscB).

The same procedure can be repeated to assay for sucrose consumption in an E. coli culture, in which sucrose is initially added to a culture of E. coli without any other carbon source, and serial measurements of sucrose in the supernatant are taken.

This experiment is performed with small volumes of each supernatant in a 96-well plate and its reader. This is necessary as the controls and standards associated are also numerous. (If you do the experiment in triplicate, you will have 6 samples for each reading - i.e, 2x3 including the controls).

Characterising O2 evolution by cyanobacteria using redox indicators


Since anaerobic conditions are vital for maximizing butanol yields, we also aim to monitor oxygen levels in the culture to ensure that the cultures are as close to anaerobic as possible, given the oxygen evolution by cyanobacteria which might make the culture microaerobic. To do this we plan to either use The Oxoid Anaerobic Indicator by ThermoFischer Scientific. It consists of a redox indicator solution in a laminated foil envelope that turns white from red under anaerobic conditions. We initially considered other redox indicators such as resazurin, however based on inputs from our mentors we chose the Oxoid indicator, given that resazurin was unlikely to work under microaerobic conditions and required highly anaerobic conditions.
The results of this experiment will also inform us of the need to use oxygen scavengers such as ascorbic acid in order to main near anaerobic conditions in the culture.
