Introduction

Cyanobacteria is the green element, both figuratively and literally, of our project. Modelling its behaviour both in a pure culture and in the co culture matters a lot to the direction we put our attention to. We’ve used FBA and MoMA to predict the growth rates and sucrose production rates of cyanobacteria, both with and without salt stress. Dr.Costas Maranas was generous enough to provide the Genome scale metabolic model for Synechococcus elongatus UTEX 2973 from Mueller et al., 2017.

Modelling Cyanobacteria for optimum sucrose production

The first hurdle we encountered while modelling cyanobacteria was simulating salt stress. Literature says simulating something like salt stress alters a lot of reactions in the organism and not all of them are straight forward or even known, so we had to make a few assumptions to make the model work.

Assumptions:

  1. sps and spp genes are a part of the UTEX 2973 Genome even though they weren’t part of the model *insert spp and sps reaction equations*
  2. cscB exports are of constant efficiency
  3. FBA is a steady-state analysis therefore the growth phase cannot be simulated (check co-culture for more information on this)
  4. Coupling biomass and sucrose accumulation and restraining cscB export to less than 100% simulates salt stress

Results:

  • Upon adding “SPP”, “SPS” and “cscB” and running FBA with an objective to maximise growth rate we obtained a maximum growth rate of 0.3033 h-1 and a sucrose production and transport flux of 0. This value is very similar to the experimental growth rate of 0.33 ± 0.05 h (paper insert) , which implies confidence in the model we’re using.
  • For the following conditions upon performing MoMA for optimised sucrose production we obtained a growth rate of 0.2012 h−1  and a sucrose output flux of 0.3079 mmol/gDW/h.
    • Conditions:
      • Salt stress of 150mM simulated by coupling sucrose accumulation and biomass production
      • CO2 uptake rate of -12.2 mmol/gDW/h [27]
      • cscB efficiency of 90% [12]

Upon performing MoMA for a range of values of CO2 uptakes and cscB efficiencies the following result was obtained:



Biomass vs CO2


Fig 1: Relationship between Growth rate and CO2 uptake rates when run at a salt conc. 150mM under varying cscB efficiencies


Observations:

  1. Biomass production rates decrease with decreasing CO2 uptake rates for a constant cscB efficiency
  2. Biomass increases for a decreasing cscB efficiency at a given CO2 intake value.
  3. The linear nature of the graph is due to the fact that FBA is generally a linear model and not a non-linear model. The assumption that cscB has a constant efficiency is also a contributing factor

Inference:

Carbon flux through the biomass reaction decreases as the efficiency of cscB increases. Since the objective function is to maximise sucrose production we can safely say cscB acts as a sucrose sink and therefore increases flux through sucrose production reaction at higher efficiencies.




Biomass vs Sucrose


Fig 2: Relationship between sucrose production and growth rate under a varying CO2 uptake rates for a salt concentration of 150mM

Observations:

  1. Sucrose production rates decrease with increasing biomass production rates
  2. Both increase with increase in CO2 uptake
  3. The linear nature is again, due to the linear nature of FBA and the fact that both of them share the same carbon source i.e. CO2

Inference:

Since the objective function is sucrose production and both biomass and sucrose share the same carbon source one can easily say they’ll be inversely proportional. Therefore, our aim is to direct carbon flux through both the reactions in a fixed ratio such that the organism lives comfortably and produces ample amounts of sucrose.




Sucrose production

This is a graphical representation of all the results and relations we have obtained from our attempts till now. We have eliminated all the metabolic states that do not agree with our MoMA results. The colour bar shows the sucrose production fluxes.

Observations for this are similar to the previous ones. This just helps us identify the region of conditions that we should be trying to replicate in the lab. Clearly sucrose production is maximum in the region with high cscB efficiency and high CO2 uptake rates.


Fig 9: A graph showing the production of sucrose for varying CO2 uptake rates and cscB efficiencies
Note - the colour bar on the right shows the mmol/gDW/h values of sucrose

As you can see from the graph sucrose production is maximum when there is a higher intake of carbon dioxide (source) and a higher cscB efficiency (sink). Ideally we would be trying to re-create the above mentioned conditions for enhanced production of sucrose whilst maintaining a significant fraction of maximum growth rates, but amplifying something that is already at 90% efficiency (cscB) is hard and to some extend unnecessary. Therefore we went a step ahead to find out other possible ways to increase sucrose production without losing much growth rates.

Identifying Over-expressions and Knockouts for enhanced sucrose production

We have used OptKnock to identify knockout targets and FSEOF to identify over-expression targets.



Targets for Over-expression

FSEOF is the go to algorithm to identify targets for amplification. Upon running FSEOF under the following conditions we obtained a few targets for over-expression.


Assumptions:

  1. Carbon Dioxide uptake rate is -12.2mmol/gDW/hr (Max value from literature)
  2. We have neglected salt stress because we were getting insensible results because of it

Sources of inaccuraies:

  1. The model is not complete
  2. The geens have no names
  3. Some reactions have no gene-protein-relations. Gene-protein-relations (GPRs) are rules that bind reactions in the organism to particular genes or combination of genes. For example, if reaction R1 requires both genes G1 and G2 then in the absence of either one of the genes the reaction seizes to work

Results:

  1. We have obtained a total of 80 reactions that can be overexpressed
  2. We have obtained a total gene count of 87. This number is a bit misleading because if we were to over express all these 80 reactions we wouldn’t have to over express all the 87 genes because some of the Gene-Protein-Relations do not require the entire set of genes that govern them
  3. These 87 genes are responsible for a total of 555 reactions in the organism. Few of them are the same reaction but in different parts of the organism, for example if the cytoplasm and periplasm both contain a reaction X, the reaction X is counted twice
  4. If we were to count X only once then we have 107 reactions governed by these 87 genes but this is not of much importance because over expressing any gene will over express all its reactions whether it is in cytoplasm or periplasm
  5. 25 out of the 80 target reactions have no gene-protein-relations so we cannot test these out in the lab
  6. Although the sucrose production reaches the theoretical maximum, the biomass flux slowly dies as this happens therefore the higher is not always the better
  7. Almost all of them follow linear behaviour

We searched through literature to find a way to represent the data we have collected, but we found no proven way to do so. Therefore this can utmost be considered as a qualitative analysis of potential targets for over expression as opposed to being a quantitative and conclusive result of targets for overexpression.

These still need in-laboratory proof for us to confirm these over expression targets.

Since we could not just leave the results as is, we ranked them in order of decreasing slope because we believe that the ones with higher slopes have a higher flux handling capacity i.e., they can hold more flux for the desired purpose. In terms of the analogy between FSEOF and traffic in a city, these are the roads that can take a good chunk of traffic away from other smaller roads and still not get jammed.

These targets of overexpression are in terms of reactions, but in wet lab we can not selectively overexpress reactions. What we can overexpress in the lab are genes. So naturally, we looked into how other reactions in the model get affected if and when we overexpress one of the above genes. We believe the problem is with the model, as it is still incomplete and not verified, and therefore have concluded nothing further can be done to produce Knockout results.
